## OAA Brand & Identity

## Cheat Sheet

```
What is Brand and Why Does it Matter?
```
The Ontario Association of Architects (OAA)
is a self-regulating organization **dedicated** to
serving the public interest while promoting and
increasing the knowledge, skill, and proficiency
of its members. It does so to **protect the users
of architecture professionals’ services** by
ensuring only qualified people are able to practise
architecture in Ontario.

Equity, Diversity, & Inclusion

The OAA has a duty to ensure the profession
responds to and reflects the diversity of people in
Ontario. It strives to use inclusive language and
visuals in all communications.

Watch for harmful assumptions about:
•	 Gender Identity, Gender Expression, Sex
•	 Sexual Orientation
•	 Race/Colour/Ancestry, Ethnic Origin,
Nationality, Citizenship, Place of Origin
•	 Language Proficiency
•	 Religious Affiliation/Creed
•	 Financial Status
•	 Marital/Family Status
•	 Physical/Cognitive Ability, Health Status
•	 Education Status
•	 Age
Further information at P:\Brand+Identity\
Additional Resources.

The OAA is equally committed to providing
accessible materials to all audiences. It
continues to make updates and to apply any and
all relevant accessibility standards. The OAA will
provide materials in an alternate format upon
request.

## OAA Logo

```
Full Logomark
```
The OAA Logo is the main visual identifier for the
Ontario Association of Architects (OAA).

The OAA logo epitomizes the broader visual style
which is **clean, minimal,** and **linear**.

Rules for Using the Logo Lockup and Symbol

There are English, French, and Bilingual*
versions of the logo lockup. For all versions, there
are two sizes: standard size and small size. They
are designed to perform best in different scales.
(Please see the complete _OAA Brand & Identity
Guidelines_ for details)

*The bilingual logo lockup should only be used
when both languages need to be represented.

The logo symbol can be used on its own to
represent the brand **if the full corporate name is
clearly visible elsewhere**. Otherwise, please use
the full logomark.

The minimum amount of clear space that must
be kept around the logo and logo symbol is equal
to 1/4 the width of the logo symbol.

**Do not separate or modify any single
component from the logomark.**

The logo can be used on different coloured
backgrounds or photographs, but should always
remain black or white and be clear and legible.

## Colours & Typography

```
Colours
```
The dominant brand colour is white which should
be used mostly as a background colour paired
with black type. It’s crisp, clean, and passes all
accessibility requirements.

Black should be used mainly for type.
It should be used sparingly for background.

When creating infographics or charts, and the
brand colours are limiting, you can use shades
of black in increments of 10%. Use other colours
sparingly and opt for muted tones.

For digital use, the colour contrasts need to be
at least level AA according to the Web Content
Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG).

Primary and Alternate Typeface

The primary typeface is Akkurat, used for the OAA
logomark, the OAA website, the official letterhead
footer, certificates, graphic designs, and official
publications.

The alternate typeface is Arial, to be used
when Akkurat is not available. Arial has many
applications including memos, internal reports,
presentations, and other standard documents.

Each typeface comes in multiple weights. Opt
for regular, and use bold or light sparingly to
create hierarchy. In most cases, hierarchy of
information should be created with white space,
instead of a variety of type sizes and weights.

as appropriate to other word processors.

```
Quick Tips for Using Microsoft Word Templates
```
```
Please remember the following:
```
```
•	 Use the accessibility features in Microsoft Word (Styles, Table of Contents, Paragraph spacing for white space);
•	 Whenever possible, ensure to add alt-text on all images and/or tables used in documents and digital platforms;
•	 Acronyms must be preceded by the full name, which should be repeated at intervals; use periods or spaces for
acronyms in alt-text;
•	 Closed captioning should be included on all new videos produced by the OAA;
•	 Minimum font size for body text must be 10 pt;
•	 Use plain language and “chunk” your content; avoid long strings of uninterrupted and dense content; and
•	 Avoid placing text on images in a way that might obscure legibility.
```
```
Please remember the following:
```
```
•	Borders and Tables should be black or medium/dark grey, line weight 1/2 pt; use borders to create a line break;
•	Use the Styles feature in your document and select appropriate headings from available options; multiple levels of
headings should follow a logical descending order;
•	Line spacing should be set to “Multiple” at 1.08 pt. Paragraphs should have a mimimum of 6 pt space after. Use
paragraph spacing or a page break to create white space;
•	Use the bullet and numbering library built into Word; choose the round black bullets as the highest level;
•	Page margins should be set to “Narrow” (0.5 inches) all around your page; to achieve a 2/3 page paragraph width, set
your right paragraph indentation to 2.25 inches;
•	Styles can be pre-formatted and applied throughout a document to ensure accessibility and brand compliance.
To avoid “mixing” styles between documents, when copying and pasting content, right click and select “Merge
Formatting” from your paste options. This will override any imported style formatting; and
•	Inches are the preferred units of measurement used in this guide and across OAA templates.
```

## Content Standards

```
Writing Style - Quick Tips
```
The following is a snapshot of some basic
content requirements. For full details, please
consult the complete _OAA Brand & Identity
Guidelines._

All representatives of the OAA must speak with a
unified voice on behalf of the organization.

The OAA is governed by the _Architects Act,_ which
has specific and binding requirements. The
content produced and disseminated by the OAA
needs to reflect and uphold our duties therein.

An inconsistent approach to messaging is at best
confusing and at worst a liability.

```
Primary Language
```
```
The majority of OAA communications are
currently conducted in English.
```
```
As language capabilities expand, relevant
updates will be reflected in future versions of the
OAA Brand & Identity Guidelines.
```
```
Editorial Style
```
```
Please remember the following:
```
```
•	 Use simple phrasing and short sentence structure;
•	 Organize content in “chunks” that are brief, to the point, and focused;
•	 Use active rather than passive voice. (ex: “The dog ate the bone,” not “The bone was eaten by the dog.”);
•	 Choose familiar, everyday words and explain technical or unfamiliar terms;
•	 Be clear, unambiguous, and accurate;
•	 Be succinct. Avoid excessive description or redundancy (ex: “The stairs are steep,” not “The stairs ascend at a highly
challenging vertical incline.”);
•	 Keep content relevant to your target audience(s); and
•	 Tone should convey leadership and authority in a friendly and balanced way.
```
```
Please remember the following:
```
```
•	In general, the OAA follows Canadian Press Style; exceptions and special rules can be found in the complete OAA
Brand & Identity Guidelines ;
•	Always italicize the words Architects Act unless the rest of the sentence is italicized - then use regular;
•	Use inclusive language and avoid binary pronouns or prefixes (i.e. he/she, Mr./Mrs./Ms.), unless known; and
•	Refer to the complete guidelines for a Glossary of Terms.
```
